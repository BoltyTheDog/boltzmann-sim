Theory

The Lattice Boltzmann Method

The Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) is a computational fluid dynamics technique used to simulate fluid flows. Instead of solving the Navier-Stokes equations directly, LBM models fluid as a collection of particles and simulates their collisions and streaming.

This method is particularly well-suited for complex geometries, multiphase flows, and flows in porous media. It's also inherently parallelizable, making it efficient for modern computing architectures.

Key Components of LBM

  • Discretized Velocity Space: The D2Q9 model used here has 9 discrete velocity directions, as shown in the diagram above.
  • Collision Step: Particles at each lattice site collide, resulting in redistribution according to the BGK approximation.
  • Streaming Step: After collision, particles move to neighboring lattice sites.
  • Boundary Conditions: Special rules handle obstacles and inlet/outlet conditions.

Reynolds Number

The Reynolds number (Re) characterizes the flow and is given by Re = (characteristic length × velocity) / viscosity. Lower viscosity results in higher Re and potentially turbulent flow patterns.

Formulas

Lattice Boltzmann Method: Key Equations

Continuous Boltzmann Equation

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + \vec{v} \cdot \nabla f = \Omega(f)$$

The Boltzmann equation describes the evolution of the particle distribution function f in phase space, where Ω is the collision operator.

Discretized LBM Equation

$$f_i(\vec{x} + \vec{c}_i\Delta t, t + \Delta t) - f_i(\vec{x}, t) = \Omega_i(f)$$

The discretized equation shows how the distribution function evolves in discrete steps, where c̲ᵢ represents discrete velocities.

BGK Collision Operator

$$\Omega_i(f) = -\frac{1}{\tau}(f_i - f_i^{eq})$$

The BGK (Bhatnagar-Gross-Krook) approximation for collisions, where τ is the relaxation time and f^(eq) is the equilibrium distribution.

Equilibrium Distribution

$$f_i^{eq} = \rho w_i \left[1 + \frac{\vec{c}_i \cdot \vec{u}}{c_s^2} + \frac{(\vec{c}_i \cdot \vec{u})^2}{2c_s^4} - \frac{\vec{u} \cdot \vec{u}}{2c_s^2}\right]$$

The equilibrium distribution function, where ρ is density, w_i are weights, u̲ is velocity, and c_s is the speed of sound in the lattice.

Macroscopic Quantities

Density

$$\rho = \sum_i f_i$$

The fluid density is the sum of all distribution functions at a point.

Momentum

$$\rho \vec{u} = \sum_i f_i \vec{c}_i$$

The fluid momentum is the weighted sum of distribution functions with their respective velocities.

Connection to Navier-Stokes

Continuity Equation

$$\nabla \cdot \vec{u} = 0$$

The continuity equation for incompressible flow, which LBM satisfies in the low Mach number limit.

Momentum Equation

$$\frac{\partial \vec{u}}{\partial t} + (\vec{u} \cdot \nabla)\vec{u} = -\frac{1}{\rho}\nabla p + \nu \nabla^2 \vec{u}$$

The Navier-Stokes equation for momentum conservation, which LBM approximates through the Chapman-Enskog expansion.

Relationship between Viscosity and Relaxation Time

$$\nu = c_s^2 (\tau - 0.5)$$

The kinematic viscosity ν in LBM is determined by the relaxation time τ and the square of the speed of sound c_s.

About

This simulator implements the Lattice Boltzmann Method (LBM) for computational fluid dynamics. The backend is powered by Go, and this frontend interface allows for interactive parameter adjustment and visualization.

The simulation creates a circular obstacle in the middle of a flow field and simulates fluid flow around it. The color intensity represents the fluid velocity magnitude.

Implementation Details

  • The D2Q9 model with 9 velocity directions is used
  • BGK collision operator with a single relaxation time
  • Periodic boundary conditions on the top and bottom
  • Inlet velocity condition on the left
  • Outflow condition on the right
  • Bounce-back boundary condition for the obstacle
